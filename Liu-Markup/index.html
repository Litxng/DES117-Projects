Introduction

The purpose of this report is to analyze the visual rhetoric of the museum and assess how well the museum/curator did in delivering the media. Based on the UC Davis Museum website, The Museum aims to enhance the teaching and research activities of the UC Davis Design Department by providing a space for design students to showcase their work and learn effective presentation techniques. Additionally, their exhibition program highlights the work of innovative designers, delves into design issues, showcases the design collection, and functions as a valuable teaching and learning resource for faculty and students in the Department of Design and throughout UC Davis. Furthermore, the goal of the current exhibition, “STILL: Racism in America, A Retrospective in Cartoons”, is to explore racism through cartoons.

From addressing those questions and going through the museum, the museum was successful on delivery media.

Interactive Elements

In his exploration of interactive elements in informal settings such as museums, Screven highlights their impact on visitor attention. He notes that visitors tend to have a higher attention span and are more engaged when interactive elements are incorporated into the museum experience. However, Screven emphasizes that these interactive elements must be relevant to the exhibition and offer a meaningful learning experience to be effective.

During my visit to the UC Davis Design Museum, I identified two interactive elements: a Spotify playlist and a board game. Figure 1 shows the Spotify playlist, located at the museum's entrance, which was curated by the exhibition designer Barbara Brandon-Croft to complement the museum viewing experience. It features songs by black artists, aligning with the purpose of the exhibition. While the concept was creative, I found the execution lacking. The poster displaying the playlist seemed somewhat plain compared to the museum's overall vibe, giving the impression of being hastily put together. Additionally, the QR code linked to a website with ads, detracted from the immersive experience.

Overall, interactive elements such as the Spotify playlist have the potential to enrich visitors' museum experience by offering a unique and engaging way to interact with the exhibition's themes. However, these elements must be thoughtfully designed and integrated into the museum's overall aesthetic and purpose to maximize their impact.

Another captivating interactive element within the exhibition is the CULLUD Board Game, Figure 2. The display includes the Cullud board, dice and figures, an enlarged version of the board, and an instruction manual. This element proved to be both engaging and educational, adding a layer of fun to the overall museum experience. Personally, it left me eager to return with a companion to enjoy the game together, showcasing how such elements can entice visitors to revisit the museum. I particularly appreciated the element's placement behind the X-shaped wall, as it felt like a hidden gem that revived my interest in the museum when my attention was starting to disappear. However, it could be argued that placing this display in a more visible location, such as the X wall facing the entrance, could attract more attention from passersby, potentially drawing in a larger audience.

In conclusion, both the Spotify playlist and the CULLUD Board Game demonstrate the potential of interactive elements to enrich visitors' museum experiences by offering engaging ways to interact with exhibition themes. For these elements to be effective, they must be carefully designed and integrated into the museum's aesthetic and purpose. Strategically placing interactive displays can not only revive visitor interest but also encourage return visits, enhancing overall engagement. As museums evolve, incorporating such innovative elements will be essential for maintaining relevance and fostering meaningful connections with audiences.

Exhibition Focus and Layout

The museum's focus on the current exhibition, "STILL: Racism in America, A Retrospective in Cartoons," was different from what I anticipated. I had expected to see a combination of student work alongside the exhibition but found that only the current exhibition was on display. This diminished my interest somewhat, as I had been particularly looking forward to seeing student work, being a student myself. Visitors who, like me, had checked the website beforehand may have shared similar feelings of disappointment. Despite this, the museum was easy to navigate, located prominently as the first room upon entering from the front entrance. The website also provided clear directions and instructions on how to get to Cruess Hall, where the museum is located. This ease of access is a significant advantage, ensuring that visitors can find and experience the museum without difficulty.

Welcoming Atmosphere

Upon entering the museum, my initial impression was that it was warm and welcoming as shown in Figure 3. The lighting, with its warm tones, created a cozy and inviting atmosphere. This aligns with Vonn's idea in "THE PSYCHOLOGY OF LIGHT: HOW DIFFERENT LIGHTING IMPACTS OUR EMOTIONS," which suggests that warm tones are associated with feelings of comfort and relaxation. As I explored the museum, I found this to be true, feeling at ease within the space. In the center of the room, there is a bench where visitors can sit and relax. During my visit, I observed another visitor reading a book and enjoying the peaceful ambiance. This scene further highlights the museum's welcoming and relaxing atmosphere.

Strategic Lighting and Color Scheme

Another notable aspect of the museum's lighting is how each light is strategically positioned to shine on the exhibition displays as shown in Figure 4. This creates a spotlight effect, drawing attention to and highlighting each display. This not only enhances the visual appeal of the exhibits but also helps guide visitors' focus and engagement. Contrasted to the darker red and black backgrounds, the spotlight lighting draws attention to the displays even more. Regarding the color scheme, the main colors for the walls are red, black, and white, matching the event poster and the black-and-white cartoon displays. The bold red background makes the displays stand out. In “First Impressions: Perception and Genres”, Hilligos talked about how powerful color is when it comes to focusing attention. From their research, they found that colors help readers find, organize, and make decisions about complex information. However, the overuse of color can also distract and overwhelm the readers. In this case, I think the museum did a good job of balancing the colors used. There wasn’t an overwhelming amount of colors being used in the space that would distract visitors from the actual displays. The colors not only match the displays aesthetically but also draw attention to them.

Texts

Compared to other museums I've visited, this exhibition featured concise description cards with only a few sentences each as shown in Figure 5(bottom). This approach made it easier for me to understand and learn about the displays compared to the long paragraphs often found in museum exhibits. The shortness of text not only suited my low attention span but also emphasized the importance of each display. This aligns with Screven's research, which suggests that visitors prefer exhibits that are concise, engaging, and relatable. Displays that "come to life," convey messages quickly, and relate to familiar experiences are more favored. Conversely, visitors dislike overly complex exhibits, lack personal connections, or feel disconnected from the real world. This highlights the appeal of short, direct texts in museums, as they are more likely to captivate visitors, aid in learning, and facilitate information retention. The display notes, often reflecting Barbara Croft's thoughts, further enhance this sense of familiarity and connection, ultimately enriching the visitor's experience.

Conclusions

In conclusion, the UC Davis Design Museum has effectively utilized delivery media and visual rhetoric to enhance visitor experiences. The strategic use of interactive elements, such as the Spotify playlist and the CULLUD Board Game, has enriched the exhibition's themes and engaged visitors. The museum's welcoming atmosphere was highlighted by warm lighting and a thoughtful color scheme, creating a comfortable environment conducive to learning and reflection. Additionally, the concise and engaging texts provide clear and impactful information, enhancing visitors' understanding and retention of the exhibition's messages.

To further improve the museum's delivery media and visual rhetoric, I recommend enhancing the Spotify playlist experience by refining its execution. This could include improving the design of the poster and ensuring that the QR code leads to a seamless and immersive experience without distractions. Additionally, considering the placement of interactive elements, such as the CULLUD Board Game, to maximize visibility and attract more visitors could enhance the overall impact of these elements.

Overall, the UC Davis Design Museum has effectively utilized various elements of delivery media and visual rhetoric to create a compelling and engaging exhibition experience. These efforts have succeeded in making the museum a valuable educational resource and a memorable destination for visitors.
